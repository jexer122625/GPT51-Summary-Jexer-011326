IEC 60825-1 is the international standard governing laser product safety, defining categories (Classes 1 through 4, plus subclasses like 1M, 2M, 3R, and 1C) based on potential hazards to eyes and skin. Its scope covers all laser products emitting radiation between 180 nm and 1 mm, ensuring manufacturers classify devices, provide warnings, and design protective measures to minimize risks.

3. Classification System Overview
IEC 60825-1 introduces a hazard-based classification system. Categories are determined by:

Accessible Emission Limits (AEL): Maximum radiation a user can access.

Exposure duration: Continuous vs. pulsed lasers.

Beam characteristics: Divergence, diameter, wavelength.

Viewing conditions: Naked eye vs. optical instruments (binoculars, microscopes).

4. Laser Categories Explained
4.1 Class 1
Definition: Safe under all normal operating conditions.

Examples: Laser printers, CD/DVD players.

Risk: No hazard to eyes or skin.

Design: Built-in shielding prevents exposure.

4.2 Class 1M
Definition: Safe for naked-eye viewing but hazardous with optical instruments.

Examples: Range-finding equipment, fiber-optic communication systems.

Risk: Magnification can concentrate beam, causing eye damage.

4.3 Class 1C (introduced in 2014)
Definition: Safe only when protective housing is intact; designed for direct application to human tissue (e.g., dermatology).

Examples: Cosmetic skin treatment lasers.

Risk: Controlled hazard; requires protective interlocks.

4.4 Class 2
Definition: Low-power visible lasers (<1 mW, 400‚Äì700 nm).

Examples: Laser pointers, barcode scanners.

Risk: Blink reflex protects eyes from accidental exposure.

Note: Intentional staring into beam is unsafe.

4.5 Class 2M
Definition: Same as Class 2 but hazardous with optical instruments.

Examples: Surveying instruments.

Risk: Magnification bypasses blink reflex protection.

4.6 Class 3R
Definition: Medium-power lasers (<5 mW visible).

Examples: High-power laser pointers, alignment tools.

Risk: Potential eye hazard if directly viewed.

Note: Lower risk than Class 3B, but stricter controls required.

4.7 Class 3B
Definition: Moderate-power lasers (5‚Äì500 mW).

Examples: Research lasers, entertainment lasers.

Risk: Direct beam hazardous to eyes; diffuse reflections usually safe.

Controls: Requires protective eyewear, restricted access.

4.8 Class 4
Definition: High-power lasers (>500 mW).

Examples: Industrial cutting/welding lasers, medical surgery lasers.

Risk: Severe eye and skin hazards, fire risk.

Controls: Strict engineering controls, interlocks, protective clothing.

5. Safety Requirements
Labeling: Mandatory warning labels with class designation.

User Information: Manuals must include hazard warnings and safe-use instructions.

Engineering Controls: Interlocks, beam shutters, protective housings.

Administrative Controls: Training, restricted access, protective eyewear.
